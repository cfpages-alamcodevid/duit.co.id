### 4. Strategic Philanthropy Frameworks (The HOW)

#### A. The Strategic Philanthropy Spectrum

| Level | Vehicle | Cost to Set Up | Tax Benefit | Control | Impact Scale | Best For |
|-------|---------|:-:|:-:|:-:|:-:|---------|
| **1. Direct Giving** | Individual donations to causes/people | Rp 0 | Limited (only to registered bodies) | Low | Low | Emergency/disaster response |
| **2. Religious Giving** | Zakat through BAZNAS/LAZ | Rp 0 | Deductible from gross income (PP 60/2010) | Low | Medium | Fulfilling religious obligation |
| **3. Program Donation** | Donation to specific charity program | Rp 0 | Deductible (max 5% net profit) | Medium | Medium | Testing philanthropic interests |
| **4. Corporate CSR** | Through company (UU PT Pasal 74) | Rp 0 | Deductible if compliant (PP 93/2010) | High | Medium | Companies with SDA-related business |
| **5. Yayasan (Foundation)** | Independent legal entity | Rp 2.5-8 juta | Highest flexibility; hibah excluded from tax | Full | High | Committed multi-year philanthropy |
| **6. Cash Waqf** | Through LKS-PWU + Nazhir | Rp 1 juta min | Tax-deductible; principal preserved | Shared | Perpetual | Long-term endowment + Islamic compliance |
| **7. Impact Investing** | Direct investment in social enterprises | Varies | Depends on structure | Full | High | Market-rate returns + impact |
| **8. Family Office Philanthropy** | Integrated with wealth management | Rp 50-500 juta | Comprehensive | Full | Highest | Multi-generational legacy |

#### B. Venture Philanthropy Model (for Indonesia)

Adapted from global venture philanthropy (Acumen, Omidyar Network) for Indonesian context:

1. **Due Diligence:** Screen social enterprises with same rigor as business investments
2. **Capacity Building:** Provide management support, not just funding
3. **Milestone-Based Funding:** Release funds based on achieved outcomes
4. **Exit Strategy:** Plan for sustainability after philanthropic funding ends
5. **Portfolio Approach:** Diversify across sectors (education, health, environment)

**Indonesian Application:** A manufacturing HNWI could fund + mentor 3-5 education technology startups, providing capital AND factory/business expertise. Returns from equity can be recycled into new investments.

#### C. Family Foundation Operating Models

**Model A: Operating Foundation** (Direct Programs)
- Foundation RUNS its own programs (e.g., Djarum Foundation runs scholarship program directly)
- Pros: Full control, brand building, deep impact
- Cons: High overhead (staff, operations), harder to scale
- Cost: 15-25% overhead on total budget
- Example: Djarum Foundation (73 employees, 5 pillars)

**Model B: Grant-Making Foundation** (Funds Partners)
- Foundation GRANTS funds to implementing organizations
- Pros: Lower overhead, easier to scale, access to expertise
- Cons: Less control, dependency on partner quality
- Cost: 5-10% overhead on total budget
- Example: Many US foundations (Gates, Ford)

**Model C: Hybrid Model**
- Mix of direct programs + grants + impact investments
- Pros: Flexibility, learning from partners while building own expertise
- Cons: Complex management, higher coordination cost
- Example: Tanoto Foundation (direct PINTAR program + grants + policy advocacy)

**Model D: Donor-Advised Fund (DAF) Equivalent** (via Platform)
- Give through existing platform (Kitabisa, BAZNAS, BWI)
- Pros: No setup cost, instant, professionally managed
- Cons: Limited control, less personal
- Cost: Platform fee ~2-5%
- Example: Kitabisa corporate giving, BWI cash waqf

#### D. Impact Measurement Framework (Moving from Outputs to Outcomes)

Based on Jakarta Post (Dec 2025) analysis by Rivandra Royono and Tanoto Foundation practice:

| Stage | What You Measure | Example |
|-------|-----------------|---------|
| **Input** | Resources deployed | Rp 500 juta scholarship fund |
| **Output** | Activities completed | 50 students received scholarships |
| **Outcome** | Changes achieved | 80% of students graduated, 70% employed in 6 months |
| **Impact** | Long-term systemic change | Reduced poverty rate in target community by 15% over 5 years |

**Practical Tool:** "Theory of Change" — map causal pathway from input → activity → output → outcome → impact. Every foundation should have one before spending Rp 1.

**Key Question from Filantropi Indonesia:** "What change do we want to see, and how will we know if it happened?"

**Data Partnership:** Tanoto Foundation partnering with Statistics Indonesia (BPS) to develop early childhood development measurement tools — shows gold standard.

### 5. Legal & Regulatory Requirements

#### A. Mendirikan Yayasan (Foundation Establishment)

```
Requirement: Yayasan (Foundation) — legal entity for philanthropy
Regulation: UU 16/2001 jo. UU 28/2004 tentang Yayasan; PP 63/2008 jo. PP 2/2013
Process:
1. Prepare founding documents (identitas pendiri, nama yayasan, maksud & tujuan)
2. Notarial deed (Akta Pendirian) — bahasa Indonesia
3. Submit to Kemenkumham via AHU Online within 10 days of deed signing
4. Obtain SK Pengesahan Badan Hukum from Menteri Hukum & HAM
5. Register NPWP
6. Publish in Berita Negara (BNRI)
Organ structure: Pembina (founder/advisors), Pengurus (management), Pengawas (supervisors)

Minimum initial endowment:
- WNI: Rp 10 million (in cash or assets)
- WNA: Rp 100 million
Timeline: 1-3 months total
Source: UU 16/2001; Kemenkumham AHU portal; notarismuda.com (2026)
```

```
Cost breakdown for establishing Yayasan:
- Notarial deed (Akta Pendirian): Rp 1.500.000 - Rp 5.000.000
- PNBP Kemenkumham (pengesahan): Rp 100.000
- AHU Online processing (notaris fee): Rp 500.000 - Rp 2.000.000
- Berita Negara (BNRI) publication: Rp 200.000 - Rp 500.000
- Supporting docs (domisili, stempel, etc.): Rp 200.000 - Rp 500.000
- NPWP: Free
TOTAL ESTIMATE: Rp 2.500.000 - Rp 8.100.000
Source: Notarismuda.com (May 2026); Valeed.id (Dec 2025)
```

```
Key Compliance Requirements for Yayasan:
1. Kekayaan yayasan terpisah dari kekayaan pribadi pendiri (Pasal 1 UU Yayasan)
2. Dilarang mengalihkan/membagikan kekayaan kepada Pembina, Pengurus, Pengawas, atau karyawan (Pasal 5)
3. If receiving > Rp 500 juta in state/foreign assistance OR having > Rp 20 M assets outside waqf: must publish annual report summary in newspaper (PP 63/2008 Pasal 18)
4. Wajib lapor tahunan ke Kemenkumham
5. Tax reporting: SPT Tahunan for Yayasan (considered a taxable entity for business income, but donations received are non-taxable if compliant)
Source: UU 16/2001; PP 63/2008; Kemenkumham
```

#### B. Tax Benefits Framework

Based on PMK 114/2025 (effective 31 Dec 2025) which consolidates:
- PP 60/2010 (zakat/religious obligations)
- PP 93/2010 (disaster, research, education, sports, social infrastructure donations)
- PP 55/2022 (tax adjustment)

```
Tax Treatment for DONORS (Pemberi):
A. Zakat and Religious Obligations:
- Deductible from GROSS INCOME (not net)
- NO 5% cap — limited by religious obligation amount
- Must be paid to BAZNAS/LAZ/registered religious body
- Must have valid proof of payment (with NPWP, amount, date, institution stamp)
- Proof needed for SPT Tahunan filing
Source: PMK 114/2025 Pasal 9; PP 60/2010

B. Non-Religious Donations (disaster, research, education, sports, social infra):
- Max 5% of previous year's net fiscal income
- Must not cause fiscal loss (tax loss carry-forward not allowed from donations)
- Must have valid proof
- Recipient must have NPWP
Source: PMK 114/2025; PP 93/2010

C. Hibah (Grant/Gift) — for RECIPIENTS:
- NOT subject to PPh (excluded from taxable income) if given to:
  - Family (1st degree direct lineage)
  - Religious bodies
  - Educational bodies
  - Social bodies (including yayasan)
  - Cooperatives
  - Micro/small business individuals
- Condition: NO business/ownership/control relationship between giver and receiver
- If transferred as asset (not cash): Capital gain from asset transfer may be exempt
Source: PMK 114/2025 Pasal 14-15
```

```
Tax Treatment for RECIPIENTS (Yayasan/Lembaga):
- Donations received: NOT taxable income (if from eligible donors and no business relationship)
- Business income of yayasan: TAXABLE (e.g., if yayasan runs a school that charges fees)
- Zakat received by BAZNAS/LAZ: NOT taxable
- Waqf returns (CWLS returns): NOT taxable if distributed for social programs
Source: PMK 114/2025; UU PPh
```

```
Practical Tax Calculation Example:
- Perusahaan dengan laba bersih fiskal tahun sebelumnya: Rp 10 M
- Maksimum sumbangan non-zakat yang deductible: 5% × Rp 10 M = Rp 500 juta
- Jika perusahaan donasi Rp 500 juta untuk pendidikan → taxable income berkurang Rp 500 juta
- With corporate PPh 22%: Tax saving = 22% × Rp 500 juta = Rp 110 juta
- Plus: Rp 500 juta contributed to social good
- Win-win: Rp 110 juta tax saving + Rp 500 juta social impact

Compare: Zakat untuk pemilik perusahaan Muslim:
- Zakat 2.5% dari penghasilan bruto — NO 5% cap
- Jika penghasilan bruto Rp 10 M → zakat Rp 250 juta deductible
- Jika tarif PPh efektif 22%: Tax saving = Rp 55 juta
Source: PP 60/2010; PMK 114/2025
```

#### C. Cash Waqf (Wakaf Uang) as Strategic Vehicle

```
Regulation: UU 41/2004 tentang Wakaf; PP 42/2006
Instruments:
- CWLS (Cash Waqf Linked Sukuk): Invested in government sukuk; principal preserved; returns for social programs
- SWR (Sukuk Wakaf Ritel): Retail version for individuals; SWR006 raised Rp 306B (Oct 2025)
- CWLD (Cash Waqf Linked Deposit): Bank deposit; 1-year min; return distributed; principal returned after term

Key Features:
- Wakaf permanen: Principal locked forever; only returns distributed
- Wakaf temporer: Fixed term; principal returned after period
- Min deposit: Rp 1 juta (CWLD); Rp 10 juta (CWLS institutional)
- Tax treatment: Wakaf is tax-deductible; returns for social programs are non-taxable
- Managed by Nazhir (waqf manager) through LKS-PWU (Islamic bank waqf unit)

Source: BWI.go.id; OJK Pedoman CWLD 2024
```

### 9. Common Myths & Misconceptions

```
Myth: "Filantropi cuma buat orang super kaya — minimal harus punya Rp 100 M."
Reality: Strategic philanthropy starts at any scale. A yayasan can be set up with Rp 10 juta endowment. The STRATEGY matters more than the SIZE. Rp 500 juta well-deployed to one cause beats Rp 5 M scattered across 10 causes.
Why it matters: The "I'm not rich enough" excuse prevents emerging HNWIs from starting early and building the "philanthropy muscle" as wealth grows.
```

```
Myth: "Lebih baik kasih langsung ke fakir miskin daripada lewat yayasan — biaya operasinya bikin dana bocor."
Reality: Professional foundations achieve LEVERAGE. One foundation staff managing Rp 5 M in scholarships can ensure the right students get funds, grades are tracked, and impact is measured. The "bocor" argument ignores that 84% efficiency (Rp 500 M of Rp 600 M return) still delivers FAR more impact than dipping into principal.
Why it matters: Direct giving is noble but rarely addresses root causes. Strategic philanthropy uses capital, expertise, and leverage for SYSTEMIC change.
```

```
Myth: "Zakat saya sudah cukup sebagai kontribusi sosial."
Reality: Zakat is a MINIMUM obligation (2.5% of income). Strategic philanthropy is ABOVE zakat — it's additional, strategic, and scalable. Think of zakat as "taxes" and strategic philanthropy as "investment allocation."
Why it matters: Many HNWIs stop at zakat and miss the full potential of leveraged, scalable, multi-generational impact.
```

```
Myth: "Kalau saya bikin yayasan, pajak saya jadi bebas."
Reality: Yayasan itself doesn't eliminate tax. Only SPECIFIC donations to the yayasan may be deductible (if they meet PP 93/2010 criteria). The yayasan is a SEPARATE legal entity with its own tax obligations. Don't confuse "yayasan as tax shelter" with "yayasan as impact vehicle."
Why it matters: Incorrect tax expectations lead to compliance risks. Strategic philanthropy focuses on IMPACT first; tax optimization is a welcome side benefit.
```

```
Myth: "Impact measurement itu buang-buang waktu — yang penting niat baik."
Reality: Without measurement, you can't tell if your Rp 500 juta is actually changing lives or just making you feel good. "Output" (berapa uang keluar) ≠ "impact" (berapa yang berubah). Even basic measurement (e.g., "80% of scholarship recipients graduated and employed") dramatically improves effectiveness.
Why it matters: HNWIs are used to ROI thinking in business. Bringing ROI thinking to philanthropy multiplies impact per rupiah.
```

### 11. Action Steps Outline

**Step 1: Audit Your Current Giving**
- How much did you give last year? (zakat, direct, donations, disaster)
- Where did it go? (religious bodies, individuals, causes)
- What was the IMPACT? (track anything?)
- Most HNWIs are shocked to discover they give 0.5-2% of income with zero tracking
- **Tools:** Simple spreadsheet; categorize by type; estimate impact
- **Writer should include:** A template table for readers

**Step 2: Define Your Philanthropic Focus (Mission Statement)**
- What problem do you care about most? (education, health, environment, poverty)
- Why that problem? (personal connection, business alignment, community need)
- Write a 1-sentence "philanthropy mission statement"
- Example: "Kami mendanai pendidikan vokasi untuk anak-anak pekerja tekstil agar putus rantai kemiskinan."
- **Involve family:** Ask spouse and children what matters to them

**Step 3: Choose Your Vehicle (Match to Goals and Scale)**

| If Your Goal Is... | Your Vehicle | Next Action |
|-------------------|-------------|-------------|
| Simple + tax-optimized giving | Donate to registered LAZ/BAZNAS | Get proper receipts for SPT |
| Test a cause before committing | Fund specific program (e.g., through Kitabisa) | Request impact report from program |
| Perpetual impact, Islamic | Cash waqf (CWLS/CWLD) | Visit LKS-PWU (Islamic bank) |
| Full control + family legacy | Yayasan (foundation) | Consult notary for deed; prepare Rp 10 M min |
| Scale existing CSR | Corporate foundation | Align with PP 93/2010; calculate 5% cap |
| Market returns + impact | Impact investing portfolio | Identify social enterprises; GIIN frameworks |

**Step 4: Set Up Legal & Tax Structure**
- For **yayasan:** Notary → Kemenkumham AHU → NPWP → BNRI (1-3 months; Rp 2.5-8 juta)
- For **cash waqf:** LKS-PWU bank → Akta Ikrar Wakaf → CWLS subscription (1-2 weeks; min Rp 1 juta)
- For **CSR-deductible donation:** Verify recipient has NPWP; get proper receipt; ensure category fits PP 93/2010
- **Critical:** Only donations to REGISTERED bodies are tax-deductible. Check DJP's latest list (PER-4/PJ/2026 updates)

**Step 5: Implement Impact Measurement**
- Start small: Answer "What change do we want to see? How will we know?"
- Track: Input → Output → Outcome → Impact
- **Year 1 goal:** Simple tracking spreadsheet
- **Year 2 goal:** Partner with university/research institution for evaluation
- **Gold standard:** Like Tanoto Foundation — fund rigorous impact evaluations, partner with BPS

**Step 6: Involve the Next Generation**
- Give children a "giving budget" (Rp 5-25 juta/year) to allocate
- Let them visit programs and see impact
- Include them in yayasan board (as Pengawas initially)
- **Research shows:** Next-gen engagement in philanthropy is the #1 predictor of multi-generational wealth preservation
- **Cultural hook:** "Gotong royong" value + "amal jariyah" concept — giving that continues after death

**Step 7: Structure for Sustainability (Endowment Model)**
- Deposit Rp 10-50 M as endowment
- Invest in conservative portfolio (deposito, bonds, Sukuk)
- Only spend RETURNS, never principal
- Target: 5-7% annual return → perpetual funding
- Add to endowment annually (e.g., 1% of business profit)
