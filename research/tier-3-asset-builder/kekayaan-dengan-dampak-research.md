### 4. Market Research (Philanthropy & Impact Landscape)

#### A. The Indonesian Philanthropy Spectrum

| Type | Characteristics | Typical Scale | HNWI Relevance |
|------|----------------|---------------|----------------|
| **Religious Giving (Zakat/Infaq/Sedekah)** | Obligation-based; 2.5% income; to BAZNAS/LAZ | Rp 32T collected (2023) | Most common; tax-deductible; but rarely strategic |
| **Direct Informal Giving** | To individuals, families, community | High prevalence (53%) | Low impact per rupiah; no leverage; unmeasurable |
| **Disaster Response Giving** | Reactive; via media/crowdfunding | Spikes during disasters | Emotion-driven; not sustained |
| **Corporate CSR** | Mandatory (UU PT Pasal 74); via company foundation | Varies by company size | Often PR-driven; minimum compliance |
| **Family Foundation** | Structured; professional management; multi-year programs | Rp 10B - 500B endowment | Highest strategic potential |
| **Impact Investing** | Market-rate or near-market returns + measurable impact | Growing in Indonesia | Undeveloped in Indonesia vs. global |
| **Cash Waqf** | Islamic endowment; principal preserved, returns distributed | Rp 2.5T+ collected (BWI data) | Emerging vehicle for HNWIs |

#### B. Strategic Philanthropy Frameworks

1. **Giving While Living (Chuck Feeney / Atlantic Philanthropies)**
   - Core idea: Give the majority of wealth during lifetime, not at death
   - "If you give while living, the money goes to work quickly — everyone gets to see the action and the results"
   - Inspired the Giving Pledge (Buffett, Gates, 2010) — now 240+ signatories
   - Relevance to Indonesia: No Indonesian billionaire has signed the Giving Pledge publicly. This is an opportunity to pioneer the conversation locally.

2. **Venture Philanthropy**
   - Applies venture capital principles to charitable giving: due diligence, milestones, capacity building, exit strategy
   - Focus on scalable social enterprises rather than traditional grants
   - Key distinction: Donors are "impact investors" not "charity givers"
   - Notable global players: Acumen Fund, Omidyar Network, Draper Richards Kaplan

3. **Impact Investing (GIIN Framework)**
   - Intentionality: Investor intends to generate positive social/environmental impact
   - Financial Return: Expectation of financial return (from below-market to market-rate)
   - Impact Measurement: Commitment to measure and report impact
   - Asset Classes: Private equity, private debt, real assets, public equities

4. **Strategic Philanthropy (Michael Porter / FSG Model)**
   - Philanthropy creates social value, but STRATEGIC philanthropy creates both social value AND strengthens the donor's competitive context
   - Example: A coffee tycoon funds coffee farmer training = better beans + better business + community impact
   - Context: Indonesia's "gotong royong" culture provides natural foundation for this approach

5. **The Effective Altruism Movement**
   - Evidence-based approach: Which interventions save the most lives per dollar?
   - Data-driven charity evaluation (GiveWell, Founders Pledge)
   - Criticized for being overly utilitarian, but introduced rigor to giving

#### C. The "Purpose-Driven Wealth" Psychology

Research shows that wealth beyond a threshold (~$2-5M net worth) stops increasing happiness. Purpose becomes the primary driver of well-being for HNWIs:

- **Eudaimonic well-being** (meaning, purpose, growth) > **hedonic well-being** (pleasure, satisfaction)
- Wealth linked to purpose reduces anxiety about loss — if your wealth serves a mission larger than yourself, market fluctuations matter less
- "Legacy motivation" — the desire to leave a lasting impact — is one of the strongest predictors of philanthropic behavior among HNWIs
- Giving activates the brain's reward centers similarly to receiving money (neuroscience research via Harbaugh, Mayr, Burghart 2007)

#### D. The Indonesian "Amal" to Strategic Philanthropy Gap

- **Current state:** Most Indonesian HNW giving is reactive (when asked by friends/family), religious (zakat 2.5%), or disaster-driven
- **Gap:** Almost no Indonesian HNWIs have: a formal philanthropic mission statement, an impact measurement framework, or a multi-year giving plan
- **Opportunity:** Position strategic philanthropy as the FINAL STEP in the wealth journey — from survival (Tier 0) → hustling (Tier 1) → scaling (Tier 2) → asset building (Tier 3) → impact/legacy (Tier 3-4)
- **Cultural fit:** "Gotong royong" and "amal jariyah" (ongoing charity) concepts align naturally with strategic philanthropy — we're translating existing values into a structured framework

### 5. Legal & Regulatory Requirements (Brief — see sibling article for full treatment)

```
Topic: Tax deductibility of donations in Indonesia
Regulation: UU PPh Pasal 6 Ayat (1); UU 23/2011 tentang Pengelolaan Zakat; PMK 114/2025
Key Rules:
- Zakat paid to BAZNAS/LAZ can be deducted from taxable income (max 2.5% of income)
- Certain donations for national disaster, R&D, education, sport, social infrastructure are deductible
- Religious contributions (zakat for Muslims, tithe for Christians) to government-recognized bodies are deductible
- Non-religious donations to formal charities may qualify under specific categories
Relevance: Strategic philanthropy can reduce tax burden while creating impact — double benefit
Source: PWC Tax Summaries Indonesia 2025; DGT official website
Quote-ready: "Zakat payments can lower taxable income — Rp 3M zakat could save Rp 150K in taxes for a 5% bracket taxpayer"
```

```
Topic: Establishment of Yayasan (Foundation) for philanthropy
Regulation: UU 16/2001 jo. UU 28/2004 tentang Yayasan
Process:
1. Establish with notarial deed
2. Obtain legal entity status from Ministry of Law & Human Rights
3. Obtain NPWP (tax ID)
4. Register with relevant authorities
Cost: Rp 5-15 million for notarial + legalization fees
Timeline: 1-3 months
Key Requirement: Foundation assets must be separated from founder's personal assets
Relevance: Proper foundation structure enables tax-efficient, multi-generational philanthropy
```

```
Topic: Cash Waqf as philanthropic vehicle
Regulation: UU 41/2004 tentang Wakaf; PP 42/2006; BWI regulations
Process: Waqf via LKS-PWU (Islamic bank waqf unit); minimum Rp 10 million
Vehicle: Wakaf uang through licensed nazir (waqf manager)
Impact: Principal preserved forever; returns distributed for social programs
Tax treatment: Waqf is tax-deductible and not subject to PPh
Relevance: Cash waqf is uniquely Indonesian-Islamic — preserves capital while generating ongoing impact
Source: BWI (Badan Wakaf Indonesia); OJK
```

### 9. Common Myths & Misconceptions

```
Myth: "Saya masih terlalu muda/miskin untuk mikirin dampak. Nanti kalau sudah kaya banget."
Reality: Impact thinking is a MINDSET, not a wealth threshold. You can start with 1% of income or 10 hours/month of pro bono expertise. The habit of strategic giving scales with wealth.
Why it matters: Building the "impact muscle" early prevents the "now what?" crisis when wealth is achieved.
```

```
Myth: "Filantropi itu cuma buat orang yang sudah mau meninggal — warisan, wasiat."
Reality: "Giving While Living" philosophy argues the opposite — give NOW to see impact, adjust strategy, and enjoy the process. Chuck Feeney gave away $8B during his lifetime, not at death.
Why it matters: Giving at death = zero learning, zero adjustment, zero joy of seeing impact.
```

```
Myth: "Kalau saya kasih langsung ke orang miskin, itu lebih efektif daripada lewat yayasan."
Reality: Direct giving helps individuals but rarely addresses root causes. A Rp 100K/month scholarship helps one student. A Rp 10B foundation endowment that trains teachers helps THOUSANDS of students per year, forever. Scale + leverage = systemic change.
Why it matters: Strategic philanthropy isn't about being "more generous" — it's about being MORE EFFECTIVE per rupiah.
```

```
Myth: "Impact investing pasti return-nya rendah. Saya lebih baik全部 sumbang aja atau全部 investasi."
Reality: GIIN data shows the majority of impact investments (89% globally) target MARKET-RATE returns. You don't have to sacrifice returns for impact. Example: investing in education technology that serves low-income students can generate 15-20% IRR while creating measurable learning outcomes.
Why it matters: The "either-or" framing keeps HNWIs from exploring the "both-and" approach of impact investing.
```

```
Myth: "Zakat saya sudah cukup. Itu kan 2.5% dari penghasilan."
Reality: Zakat is a MINIMUM obligation, not a maximum aspiration. Strategic philanthropy goes ABOVE zakat — allocating additional time, expertise, and capital. Think of zakat as "tax compliance" and strategic philanthropy as "investment allocation."
Why it matters: Many HNWIs stop at zakat and miss the opportunity for leveraged, scalable impact.
```

### 10. Action Steps Outline

**Step 1: Define Your "Impact Purpose" (The Why)**
- Reflect: Beyond financial security, what problem do you want to solve?
- Write a 1-paragraph "philanthropy mission statement"
- Involve family (spouse, children) in the conversation
- "Jika bisnis Anda menjawab pertanyaan 'bagaimana cara menghasilkan uang', filantropi Anda harus menjawab 'untuk apa uang ini?'"

**Step 2: Audit Your Current Giving**
- How much did you give last year? (zakat, direct, disaster, etc.)
- Where did it go? (religious orgs, individuals, causes, etc.)
- What was the IMPACT of that giving? (measured or estimated)
- Most HNWIs are shocked to discover they give 0.5-1% of income in a scattered way — with no tracking

**Step 3: Choose Your Vehicle**
- **Level 1 (Simple):** Increase zakat/charity to a specific BAZNAS/LAZ program; request impact reports
- **Level 2 (Structured):** Set up a Yayasan (foundation) with clear focus and governance
- **Level 3 (Strategic):** Endowment + impact investing + family office integration
- **Level 4 (Transformational):** "Giving While Living" — allocate significant % of wealth during lifetime

**Step 4: Measure Impact, Not Just Output**
- Output = "Rp 500 juta tersalurkan untuk beasiswa"
- Impact = "50 mahasiswa dari keluarga prasejahtera lulus tepat waktu, 80% mendapat pekerjaan dalam 6 bulan"
- Use frameworks: Theory of Change, Logic Model, IRIS+ (GIIN)

**Step 5: Involve the Next Generation**
- Give children a "giving budget" to allocate
- Let them visit grantees and see impact firsthand
- Research shows: Next-gen engagement in philanthropy is the #1 predictor of multi-generational wealth preservation
- "If your children don't understand WHY your wealth exists, they WILL lose it in 3 generations" (tie to article 3.158 — Kekayaan yang Lenyap)

**Step 6: Structure for Sustainability**
- Endowment model: Principal preserved, only returns distributed
- Professional management: Don't run foundation like a family business
- Governance: Board with external advisors, not just family members
- Sunset clause: Will the foundation exist forever or spend down? Both are valid — decide consciously
